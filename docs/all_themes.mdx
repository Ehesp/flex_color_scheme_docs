can be turned into themes.

and dark color schemes. You you can use them in your applications if you like.

## Built-in Scheme Designs

The built-in color schemes are all tuned matching light and dark scheme pairs.
Most of them are pretty conservative, but some are intentionally a bit more
playful and bold. The first two color schemes are actually the Flutter and
Material 2 Design Guide examples of light and dark color schemes, and the Flutter
high contrast light and dark color versions as well, with some Material 3 color
additions. The color scheme "Amber blue" is intended as a
high-contrast theme, as an alternative to the built-in Material
high contrast theme.

The built-in schemes do not claim to be a "this selection" fits all needs
final word. Nor do they claim to be more correct than any other color scheme.
What fits your needs and looks good to you, is the right choice for your
application and use case.

## Custom Color Schemes

You can make your own custom scheme totally from scratch, or use the built-in
ones as a starting point and inspiration. You can re-use colors from existing
schemes, via their const `FlexColor` name. All the color values in the color
schemes are available as const values, so you can easily make new custom
combinations using existing color scheme colors and add a few custom color
values to the mix.

In the tutorial, based on examples 1 to 5, we show how you can make, and use your own
custom color schemes with FlexColorScheme. Maybe the built-in examples will
inspire your creative side to define your own beautiful color schemes. The tutorials walk
you through how to define your custom color schemes and make themes based on them.

You can also create your own custom list of schemes, then use this list with
only your own custom color schemes selection. You may also append some, or
even all the built-in schemes after your custom schemes, and give your
users a lot of theming options. How to do this is demonstrated in the tutorial
with examples 4 and 5.

## Background

Where did the built-in color schemes come from? While building, testing and
using this package over a long period of time, a number
of color schemes were born as a side product while I tested it. I decided to
include them in FlexColorscheme and offer them for re-use and inspiration
as they are. It is however not necessary to use them to get the
benefits of FlexColorScheme. Originally I even though it would just be the engine
without any pre-made colors, but it is much more fun and engaging to use the
package with some starting point that can modified as desired.

## Use Cases

Even if you most likely want, need or must use some given
colors for customer projects or your the nex big startup venture.
For getting things started using some quick pre-made themes is great time
saver when you have other things to focus on first. I have also found that
it is a nice tool to use to just try an app your are working on with many
different options to see how it feels.

With the launch of Android 12 and dynamic themes, we will se an increasing
expectation form users to be able to customize the look of consumer apps completely.
FlexColorScheme gives you a large selection of option that you can offer out of the box
as pre-made choices, in addition to whatever main brand color you have as default.
FlexColorScheme also supports using dynamic themes and can even generate them itself
using key colors to make seeded ColorSchemes.

As advanced theme fiddling options you can even offer toggles for users to play with, like
swapping main and secondary colors, reducing the amount of used colors from the theme.
Even the option to auto-tune built-in themes by turning on seed generated themes using their
colors as key colors, with maybe even two or three tone options for the auto tuning.
The possibilities are limitless. The **Themes Playground** app, which is also
tutorial example 5, shows you how this is done, it is remarkable simple.
